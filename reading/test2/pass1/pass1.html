Starting school later has positive effects on teens

A growing body of evidence demonstrates that adolescents benefit from more sleep.

With classes that start as early as 7 a.m. and buses that arrive long before sunrise, around 80% of U.S. children in grades 6–12 are not getting the recommended amount of sleep, according to research by the National Sleep Foundation. These early start times contribute to irritability, depression, poor grades, weight gain, and increased caffeine consumption.

Fueled by accumulating research showing that teens are biologically designed to sleep late, many U.S. schools are beginning the day later. Others resist, worried about bus schedules, after-school programs, or that children may just stay up later.

According to Kyla Wahlstrom, director of the Center for Applied Research and Educational Improvement at the University of Minnesota, despite differences in race and socioeconomic factors between two school districts, positive changes appeared immediately after adjusting start times.

Students were more alert, cafeterias were calmer, and there were fewer fights. With nearly an hour more sleep each night, students reported feeling less depressed. Parents also said their children were easier to live with.

The melatonin shift

As children approach puberty, their bodies begin releasing melatonin—the hormone responsible for sleepiness—about two hours later than before.

Teens cannot fall asleep before around 11 p.m., yet still need 9.25 hours of sleep. A major period of sleepiness occurs between 5–7 a.m. for teens and 3–5 a.m. for adults, meaning teens are biologically programmed to sleep when school asks them to wake.

Chronic sleep deprivation harms immunity and increases hunger hormones, possibly contributing to obesity.

Many teens turn to caffeinated drinks. In a Massachusetts high school, 95% consumed caffeine in the previous two weeks—mostly sodas, often in afternoons or evenings. Caffeine remains in the body up to five hours, reduces sleep quality, and may affect developing brains, though research is incomplete.

Schools respond

As research grows, more schools are shifting to later starts. Though not officially tracked, inquiries from districts continue to increase. One conclusion is clear: teens need adequate sleep, and changing school start times helps.
